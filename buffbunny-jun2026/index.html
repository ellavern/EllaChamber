Key Takeaways

🔁

Shift new spend toward the winning cluster, don't just cut the underperformer

Creative #1 by spend is consuming $5.2K at a 9.8x ROAS. Rather than pulling that budget entirely, direct incremental spend toward the winning cluster (21–31x ROAS) while keeping a range of active creatives in rotation. Meta's delivery needs variety to avoid fatigue, so the move is "add more winners," not "narrow the set down to one."

Immediate action
📈

The proven creative formula is working, so let's produce more of it

The winning cluster (specifically the video creatives with measurable attention signals) consistently returns 21–31x ROAS at $5–8 CPA. These creatives have a clear, repeatable pattern that the creative team can brief against. The pipeline should be oriented to refresh and scale this format.

Creative direction
🔍

Give the Black Friday hook a real test budget

The 72% thumbstop and 60% hold rate are the strongest attention signals in the account right now. Our plan is to keep this video leading the sale push and let the statics carry retargeting, so we can confirm the hook converts as well as it stops the scroll.

Testing priority

Our suggestion is to keep shifting spend from underperformers toward proven winners as the fastest lever available for growth right now.

📸 Top Visual Trends

What the top ROAS and CPA creatives have in common visually
Video carries the winning cluster. 3 of the top 6 creatives by ROAS are video, and the winning group as a whole shows measurable thumbstop data where available.
Clean framing wins, even with different subjects. The top creative (73.3x) is a group shot of women photographed from behind. The 36.4x creative uses a single model instead. Both keep the background uncluttered and the product the clear focal point.
Cheaper, more relevant impressions. The winning cluster runs at roughly a $12 to $15 CPM against a $13.30 account average, which suggests the visuals themselves are earning better placement.

💬 Top Messaging and Copy Trends

What the top ROAS and CPA creatives have in common in their copy
Little to no headline copy. None of the top 6 ROAS winners rely on a text overlay to do the selling. The product and the person wearing it carry the message on their own.
Product-first, not story-first. Winners get to product clarity fast, which lines up with their high click-to-purchase rates of 20 to 47 percent.
No discount language required. The top ROAS creative is converting at 73.3x with no sale callout at all.
Where copy is present, simple and collection-forward wins. Top performers share the same simple callout ("New Summer Essentials Collection"), since they're promoting the same collection drop.
✅
This is what we're planning to do
We're planning to hand our design team a static brief built on the winning formula: single subject, clean/uncluttered background, little to no text overlay, product front and center in the first frame. Where a headline is used at all, we're planning to keep it simple and collection-forward and retire specific, clinical, or negative-framed call-outs, which are the account's most consistent underperformers. We'll ask for 3 to 4 concept variations so there's enough volume to confirm the pattern holds.

📸 Top Visual Trends

What is showing up across all 7 competitor libraries
UGC and mirror-selfie formats dominate. Gymshark and AYBL both list UGC Selfie among their top 3 formats, leaning on real-person, gym-mirror content to prove fit and shaping claims.
Discount banners are their own visual format for the value brands. Halara's single largest format, at 34 percent of its library, is a prominent discount banner treatment at high volume.
Feature callouts layered directly on the product shot. Oner Active and AYBL both use on-image labels pointing to specific construction details, like a waist snatch or glute lift.

💬 Top Messaging and Copy Trends

What is showing up in competitor copy and offers
Two distinct playbooks are running side by side. Value brands like Halara lead hard with percent-off and multi-buy offers, while Gymshark, Lululemon, Athleta, AYBL, and YoungLA mostly do not lead with a discount at all.
Identity and drop culture over performance claims. Gymshark's "On Gym Days We Wear Pink" and YoungLA's "New Drop" both sell the feeling of owning the piece rather than a technical benefit.
Fit and shaping claims are explicit where the product is the differentiator. Oner Active and AYBL both use direct, benefit-forward headlines about how the garment holds or shapes the body.
✅
What we're planning to do: segment competitor learning by BuffBunny's actual price and brand position
BuffBunny sits closer to Gymshark, AYBL, and Oner Active than to Halara or Athleta on price and positioning, so we're planning to borrow the color-drop, duo-selfie, and on-image feature callout patterns from that group first. Our plan is to test one duo or friend-pair selfie concept and one feature callout concept next sprint.

Meta does not publicly disclose competitor spend or performance, so this section focuses on format and messaging patterns rather than ROAS or CPA.

What to Make Next

This is what we're planning to do next sprint, and our suggestions for your team

🔥 Make More Of These

Here's what we're planning to do, replicating the patterns from your top-performing cluster
Video creatives with a hard visual hook in the first frame. The winning cluster all have thumbstop data and dramatically outperform non-video formats. The hook is what separates them.
Product-focused formats that link fast to purchase. The winners show click-to-purchase rates of 20–47%. These creatives are clearly making it very easy to get to checkout.
Testimonial or social-proof variations. The highest-ROAS creative at 73x is showing a proven concept that could be iterated on. We're planning to make 3–4 variations with different hooks but the same core structure.
New strategic test: urgency-based offers for new-customer acquisition. Current pain-point-led content is underperforming for cold audiences, so we're planning to test a shift toward urgency (limited-time, limited-stock, deadline-driven) creative aimed specifically at new customers.

🧪 Tests to Run This Sprint

Here's what we're planning to test, concrete experiments worth budget and effort right now
Budget test on the 72% thumbstop creative. We're planning to give it $300–500. If the hook is converting even at the mid-range CPA ($10–12), that's a new winner to scale.
Iterate on the 73x ROAS creative. This is the account's most efficient ad. We're planning to make 3 angle variations (different hook, same product/structure). Even if none hit 73x, landing at 30–40x at higher spend would be a major win.
Urgency-offer concepts for cold traffic. We're planning to brief 2–3 new-customer concepts built around a countdown, limited-stock callout, or deadline-driven offer.
Reduce, don't zero out. Our plan is to trim spend on the $5.2K underperformer and pause the two smallest, clearest losers (2.4x and 1.2x ROAS), then redirect that budget across the winning cluster as a group.
